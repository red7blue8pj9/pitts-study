AdvertisementSupported byBy The New York TimesThe New York Times put journalists on the ground in more than 150 countries over the last year, covering nearly every corner of the world. Here are some of the most gripping tales from our foreign correspondents, photographers and editors in 2016.Mike Ives and Josh Haner took us to Tarawa, Kiribati, where climate change is affecting the lives of the people there and even the tiny island nation’s existence.Jim Yardley reported from a squalid refugee camp in Idomeni, Greece, on the tens of thousands of people trapped in the country last spring as aid groups warned of a potential humanitarian crisis.Andrew Higgins reported from Wigan, England, a Labour-dominated city that voted “Leave” last summer to jolt officials in London as well as in Brussels into restoring a lost world of secure jobs and communities.Bryan Denton was with Iraqi counterterrorism forces as they began pushing toward Mosul in October. He wrote a first-person account from Bartella of being wounded in an explosion.When Diaa Hadid made a pilgrimage to Mecca, Saudi Arabia, this year, she took us along with her as she detailed her personal journey.Ellen Barry, the South Asia bureau chief, spent five months reporting in Peepli Khera, Meerut, Alipur, Atrara and Ganeshpur, India, on a group of women who challenged a patriarchal social order, strict caste rules and centuries of tradition for the opportunity to work.Daniel Berehulak took us inside President Rodrigo Duterte’s brutal antidrug campaign in Manila, documenting 57 homicide victims over 35 days. Miaomiao Lake Village is just one of the new communities in China built to accommodate the world’s largest environmental migration project. But residents are struggling to adapt, reported Edward Wong and Mr. Haner.Nicholas Casey and Meridith Kohut reported from a state-run psychiatric hospital in Barquisimeto, Venezuela, that has not employed a psychiatrist in two years. The hospital has almost no drugs left for its tormented patients, let alone food and clothing, amid the nation’s economic crisis.Dan Levin reported from Smithers, British Columbia, an isolated northern Canadian town, on a remote highway where young girls kept disappearing.Looking for more stories?
